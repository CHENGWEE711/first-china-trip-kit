## Day 2 - Reddit long answer

**Content ID:** `day2_payment_backup_reddit`

**Suggested title:** What I would prepare before relying on mobile payments during a first China trip

The safest approach is to think in layers instead of asking whether one app will definitely work.

Before the flight, I would install Alipay and WeChat from the official app stores, prepare at
least two international cards if possible, confirm that the bank can send verification messages,
and keep the bank app accessible. I would also carry a physical card and a small RMB cash backup.

On arrival, I would not make a taxi or restaurant the first payment test. A better test is a
small purchase at a convenience store near the hotel. That gives you time to check whether the
charge reached the intended card and whether the app asks for another verification step.

Useful backups:

- working roaming or eSIM plus hotel Wi-Fi;
- screenshots of the hotel name and Chinese address;
- a physical bank card for hotels or larger venues where accepted;
- cash for a failed QR payment or data outage;
- the hotel front desk as a practical help point.

If a card cannot be added, check the name format, bank security notification, SMS delivery,
and network connection before retrying. Do not upload passport or card details through links
sent by strangers. Use only official in-app verification.

No setup is guaranteed, and support can change. I put the full first-day decision process and

## Day 2 - Quora long answer

**Content ID:** `day2_payment_setup_quora`

**Suggested question:** How should a foreign tourist prepare to pay in China?

A foreign visitor should prepare a payment stack rather than depend on a single app or card.

Start by installing Alipay and WeChat before departure. If the official setup flow supports your
card and account, add an international card and complete only the verification requested inside
the official app. Keep your bank app and the phone number used for verification available.

Then prepare two independent backups: a physical bank card and a small amount of RMB cash.
Card acceptance is more common at hotels and larger venues than at every small merchant, so a
physical card should not be your only local payment plan.

After checking into the hotel, test a small convenience-store purchase. Confirm that the charge
appears correctly before relying on the app for taxis, attractions, or dinner. Save the hotel
address in Chinese and keep mobile data plus offline screenshots ready.

If payment fails, check the network, switch the linked card, look for a bank security alert, and
ask whether the merchant expects you to scan their QR code or show your payment code. If that
still fails, use cash, a physical card where accepted, or ask the hotel for practical help.

Payment-app support, card acceptance, and verification rules may change. The detailed checklist
and troubleshooting table are here:

## Day 5 - Quora long answer

**Content ID:** `day5_240_hour_transit_quora`

**Suggested question:** How can I check whether a 240-hour China transit plan may work for my trip?

Treat the policy as a five-part verification task, not as a general promise of visa-free entry.

Check your passport nationality against the current official eligibility information. Confirm
that you hold an onward ticket to a third country or region, not simply a return to the place you
departed from. Verify that both the entry and exit ports participate in the policy and that every
hotel city stays inside the permitted travel area for that port.

Finally, verify the route with current primary sources before paying for non-refundable travel.
Useful sources include China's National Immigration Administration, the relevant Chinese embassy
or consulate, the airline or transport operator, and official port notices when available.

Examples can help explain route logic, but they do not prove that your own passport, ticket,
ports, and travel area qualify. This is especially important when combining flights, rail, and
day trips.

## Day 5 - Reddit long answer

**Content ID:** `day5_transit_route_check_reddit`

**Suggested title:** Five checks I would complete before booking a China transit route

I would not book a 240-hour transit route based only on a social post, route map, or nationality
list. I would verify five items:

1. Passport nationality is currently eligible.
2. The confirmed onward ticket continues to a third country or region.
3. The entry and exit ports are covered.
4. Every planned city stays inside the permitted travel area.
5. Current official sources support the route before non-refundable booking.

A route such as Country A to China to Country B may fit the transit logic, while Country A to
China and back to Country A may not. But route logic alone is not approval. The port, ticket,
passport, and allowed region still need separate checks.

Keep printed or offline copies of the onward ticket and hotel details. Avoid a tight connection
that leaves no time for immigration processing or a station transfer. If one condition is unclear,
do not rely on the policy yet.
